The 2-Hour Rule: Why Powersport Leads Die Before Lunch

· April 29, 2026 · 9 min read · Patrick Laperrière

It's 10:47 a.m. on a Monday. Your sales manager finally sits down with his coffee and opens the lead inbox.

There are 23 unread leads. Six are from Friday afternoon. Eleven are from Saturday. Five are from Sunday. One is from Monday morning, eighteen minutes ago.

Your sales manager is going to work through these. He'll call them in the order they came in. By noon, he'll have made it through about half. The rest will get a callback later in the afternoon, or maybe tomorrow.

By the time he reaches the lead from Friday at 4:53 p.m. — the one who was looking at a 2025 Ski-Doo MXZ Adrenaline 600R E-TEC — that customer has already booked a test ride at the dealership down the road. The deal is gone. Your sales manager will leave a voicemail. The lead will be marked "no answer" and get filed.

This scene plays out in powersport dealerships across Quebec and Ontario every single Monday morning. And it's costing you more deals than any other single thing on your sales floor.

The data that should keep you up at night

A landmark Harvard Business Review study analyzed 1.25 million sales leads received by 29 B2C and B2B companies. The findings are brutal:

  • Companies that contacted leads within an hour were nearly 7 times more likely to qualify them than companies that responded even one hour later.
  • Companies that contacted leads within 5 minutes were 21 times more likely to qualify them than those who waited 30 minutes.
  • The average response time across the companies studied was 42 hours.

Forty-two hours. Almost two full days.

These numbers aren't from the powersport industry specifically, but the principle is universal: leads decay fast. A buyer who fills out a form is in active shopping mode. They're probably on three other tabs comparing options. The dealership that gets to them first while they're still warm wins disproportionately.

In powersport, this is even more pronounced because of two things:

  1. Big-ticket emotional purchases. A snowmobile or a side-by-side isn't a planned multi-month decision the way a car often is. It's frequently impulse-driven by weather, social events, or a friend's new toy. The buying window can close in days.
  2. Limited inventory. When a buyer is interested in a specific 2025 unit, there might be three of them in their entire province. The first dealer to lock them down wins.

Why this happens at most dealerships

It's not because your sales team is lazy. It's because of how the dealership is structured.

Your salespeople are on the floor. A walk-in customer is in front of them, a deal is being negotiated, the parts counter is calling because they need a price approval. The internet leads are an afterthought. They get checked when there's a quiet moment — which on a Friday afternoon in May, never happens.

Most dealerships handle leads in one of three ways:

Option 1: One salesperson is "in charge of internet leads."
Sometimes it works. Usually that person is also closing in-store deals, and the leads get triaged into "maybe later." When they go on vacation, the leads stop being touched entirely.

Option 2: A round-robin email sends leads to all salespeople.
Diffusion of responsibility. Everyone assumes someone else is going to call. The lead gets touched by no one for 18 hours.

Option 3: The sales manager handles them.
Same person who's running the floor, doing TT&L, signing off on deals. Leads get the leftover time, which on busy days is zero minutes.

None of these scale during the busy season. And busy season in powersport is exactly when fast lead response matters most.

The math of waiting

Let me put concrete numbers on this.

Suppose your dealership receives 200 internet leads per month during peak season. Industry data suggests roughly 20-25% of well-handled powersport leads convert to a sale within 90 days. Let's use 22% as a reasonable target.

  • 22% of 200 = 44 sales per month at full conversion potential.

Now let's say your average response time is 24 hours instead of under 2 hours. Conservative academic estimates suggest a 50-60% drop in conversion when response time falls in that range.

  • 22% × 0.45 (drop factor) × 200 = ~20 sales per month.

You just lost 24 deals per month. At an average powersport unit margin (let's call it $1,200-2,500 net depending on F&I attach), you're leaving $30,000-$60,000 per month on the table. Annualized, that's a quarter to half a million dollars.

This is not a marketing problem. This is not an inventory problem. This is a process problem that 99% of dealers underweight because the lost revenue is invisible.

What "fast" actually means

The realistic target for a powersport dealership in Quebec or Ontario is:

  • Initial automated response: under 2 minutes, in the customer's language, acknowledging the lead and giving a clear next step.
  • First human contact attempt: under 2 hours during business hours, with a phone call (not just an email reply).
  • Follow-up cadence: 5 attempts over 14 days before marking as cold. Industry research suggests most dealers stop after one or two.

That's the bar. Most dealers aren't even close.

The four things that actually fix this

Here's what works, in order of impact.

1. Automated instant response (in the customer's language)

The moment a lead form is submitted, the customer should get a personalized email within 60 seconds. Not a generic "thanks for your inquiry." Something like:

"Hi [Name], thanks for asking about the 2025 [Unit]. I'm [Salesperson], I work at [Dealership], and I'll personally call you within the hour. If you'd rather text or you want to grab a slot directly, here's my calendar: [link]."

This single change does two things: it tells the customer you're not asleep at the wheel, and it buys you the breathing room to make the actual call without losing them to a competitor.

If your lead is in French, this email needs to be in French. Period. Not "Bonjour, your form has been received." Real French.

2. Lead routing that doesn't depend on a human deciding

When a lead comes in, the system should:

  • Detect the language of the form submission.
  • Detect the unit category (motorcycle, ATV, snowmobile, side-by-side, watercraft).
  • Route the lead to the right salesperson based on category specialty and current load.
  • Notify them by SMS, not just email.
  • Start a timer. If the lead isn't actioned within X minutes, escalate to the manager.

Your DMS or CRM should be doing this. If it isn't, you have a configuration problem or a software problem.

3. After-hours coverage

A weekend lead at 7 p.m. on Saturday is not a lead you can call right away. Fine. But it absolutely is a lead you can:

  • Send an automated SMS to within minutes.
  • Schedule for first-thing Monday morning, with a calendar invite already pushed to the salesperson.
  • Pre-qualify with a chatbot or form-driven flow that gathers financing info, trade-in details, timing.

By the time Monday morning hits, that lead should already have a salesperson assigned, a slot booked, and pre-qualification done.

4. Tracking that creates accountability

You can't fix what you don't measure. At minimum, your dealership should be tracking:

  • Time to first response per lead.
  • Time to first phone contact per lead.
  • Number of follow-up attempts before close or close-out.
  • Conversion rate by salesperson and by lead source.
  • Conversion rate as a function of response time.

When you put these numbers in front of your sales team weekly, behavior changes. When the numbers stay invisible, behavior doesn't.

What this looks like when it works

I've watched dealers cut their average response time from 18 hours to under 90 minutes and double their close rate within one season. Not a 10% improvement. Double.

The thing is, it doesn't take a massive investment in headcount. It takes:

  • A website that captures lead data cleanly and routes it instantly.
  • A CRM (or DMS-integrated lead manager) that automates the right things.
  • A weekly review of response-time metrics.
  • A leadership decision that this is now non-negotiable.

The technology has been available for years. The reason most dealerships don't do this is because no one inside the dealership has the time to set it up properly, and the website provider treats lead handling as out-of-scope.

The takeaway

If you remember one thing from this article, remember this: the lead you got at 4:47 p.m. Friday is worth dramatically more if you touch it before 5:30 p.m. Friday than if you touch it at 10:30 a.m. Monday.

Not 20% more. Not 50% more. Multiples more.

Most dealers know this intuitively but don't have the infrastructure to act on it. That's where the real opportunity is — not in spending more on ads, not in chasing more leads, but in not wasting the ones you already have.
