Why Most Powersport Dealer Websites Are Stuck in 2012

· May 6, 2026 · 11 min read · Patrick Laperrière

I've spent a decade in powersport. Five years on the floor, five years running the digital side at Laperrière Motosport. In that time I've onboarded onto four different dealer website platforms, three different DMS systems, and watched our industry get steadily lapped by every adjacent retail vertical.

Here's the uncomfortable thing nobody at OEM dealer meetings wants to say out loud: most powersport dealer websites in Quebec and Ontario haven't meaningfully evolved since around 2012. Some of the templates literally haven't been redesigned. Some of the inventory carousels still use the same JavaScript libraries from a decade ago. Some of the lead forms still ask for "Mr./Mrs./Miss" in a 2026 dropdown.

This isn't because dealers don't care. It's because the platform vendors who dominate this space have no real incentive to change, and dealers don't have the time, budget, or technical depth to push back. The result is a generation of dealer websites that look modern at first glance — slick photography, glossy hero videos — but break apart the moment a real customer tries to use them.

This article is for dealer principals and GMs who suspect their website is underperforming and want to understand what "stuck in 2012" actually means in concrete terms. Then we'll talk about what 2026 should look like.

What "stuck in 2012" actually means

It doesn't mean the site looks ugly. Most of these sites pass the squint test. The problem is underneath — in the architecture, the speed, the lead handling, the mobile experience, the way inventory updates flow through, the way data is captured.

Here are the seven signs your dealer website is still running on a 2012 playbook.

1. The mobile experience is an afterthought

Walk into any powersport dealership in 2026 and look at how customers shop. They're on their phone. In the showroom, in your competitor's showroom, on the couch at 9 p.m. Sunday night. Over 70% of powersport buyer research traffic now happens on mobile.

Yet most platform-built dealer sites are still designed desktop-first and squeezed into a responsive layout afterward. The signs are obvious once you know what to look for:

  • Inventory filter sidebars that get buried in a hamburger menu and stop working
  • Vehicle photo galleries that don't swipe properly on touch
  • Click-to-call buttons that aren't actually click-to-call
  • Forms with 14 fields stacked vertically that take three full scroll-screens to fill out
  • Page weights of 8-12 MB that take 6+ seconds to load on a 4G connection

This is fixable. It's been fixable since 2017. But platform providers fix it slowly — when they fix it at all — because their codebase is shared across thousands of dealers and any change risks breaking somebody.

2. Inventory updates that aren't actually real-time

Here's the test: sell a unit on Saturday afternoon. How long until that unit comes off your website?

For most dealers I've talked to, the answer is "Monday morning, when somebody manually marks it sold in the DMS, and then waits for the nightly sync." That's 36-48 hours during peak season where leads are coming in on a unit you no longer have.

A modern setup pulls inventory from your DMS continuously — every 15 minutes minimum, ideally event-driven so the moment a sale closes the unit drops off the floor and disappears from the site. The technology to do this has existed for years. Most platforms still don't.

3. Lead forms designed by lawyers, not by humans

Look at your current lead form. Count the fields. Now go look at the lead form on a Tesla configurator, or on a modern furniture e-commerce site, or on a BDC tool that actually converts.

The pattern is the same: the modern form asks for the absolute minimum upfront, then layers in additional questions only after the customer has already committed. Phone or email. That's it. Make-model-year is auto-populated from the unit page. Trade-in details come later. Financing info comes later. Anything optional is genuinely optional.

What does the typical dealer website still have? Mandatory fields for first name, last name, address line 1, address line 2, city, postal code, phone, email, "how did you hear about us," and a captcha that fails on mobile half the time.

Every additional required field reduces conversion. The data on this is overwhelming. And every dealer principal I show this to nods, agrees, and then nothing changes because the platform doesn't allow easy field-level customization.

4. Bilingual handling that's a liability, not a feature

I've written about this in detail in True Bilingual Dealer Websites, so I'll keep it short here. If you serve Quebec or bilingual Ontario regions and your "FR" button just runs the page through a switcher with no separate URLs, no hreflang tags, no real French SEO, and no language-aware automated emails — you don't have a bilingual website. You have an English website with a French costume on. Google knows. Quebec customers know. Bill 96 increasingly cares.

5. SEO that targets nothing

Open your website's pages and check the actual <title> tags and meta descriptions. For most platform sites, every category page reads something like:

"Used Inventory | YourDealershipName Powersports"

That's not SEO. That's a placeholder. Real SEO targets specific search intent — what your customers actually type into Google. "2025 Polaris Sportsman 570 dealer Trois-Rivières." "Used snowmobile dealer Laval." "Yamaha YXZ side-by-side Outaouais." If your titles, headings, and content aren't intentionally written around what people search for, your website is invisible to anyone who doesn't already know your dealership name.

This is the single highest-ROI fix on most dealer websites. And it's almost never done correctly because the platform's content management system makes it cumbersome and the dealer's marketing person doesn't know what to do with it.

6. No actual lead routing or response automation

I covered this in The 2-Hour Rule: Why Powersport Leads Die Before Lunch. The short version: most platform-built dealer sites collect leads into a generic email inbox. No language detection, no category routing, no SMS alerts, no automated acknowledgment to the customer, no follow-up cadence. The lead lands in someone's inbox and waits to be noticed.

Compare this to how a Toyota dealer or a Best Buy or a furniture e-commerce store handles a lead today. The contrast is humiliating. And it's not because the technology is hard. It's because the platforms haven't been forced to build it.

7. Hosting and performance built for 2012 traffic patterns

Most dealer platforms still run on traditional shared hosting infrastructure. Their pages load slowly, their images aren't properly compressed or served as modern formats (WebP, AVIF), they don't use a real CDN edge network, they don't preload critical resources, and their JavaScript bundles are bloated with legacy code.

Google's Core Web Vitals — the speed and interactivity metrics that directly affect your search ranking — penalize this directly. Most dealer websites I audit fail Core Web Vitals on mobile. Failing means lower rankings, which means fewer leads, which means lost sales. None of which appears on any dashboard the dealer ever sees.

Why the big platforms can't (or won't) fix this

There are essentially three or four major dealer website platforms operating in the powersport space across North America. They're all profitable. They have hundreds or thousands of dealer customers. From their point of view, things are working fine.

Here's the structural problem: a platform serving 2,000 dealers cannot easily customize the experience for any one of them. Their codebase has to work for everyone. Changing the lead form requires a product release that has to be regression-tested across every customer. Adding language-aware email automation requires building infrastructure that 60% of their customers (the US ones) don't need. Improving SEO performance requires letting individual dealers customize page metadata, which means more support tickets, which means cost.

The platform's incentive is to keep things consistent and supportable, not to maximize any individual dealer's results. That's not a moral failure — it's just the math of running a SaaS company. But the consequence is that any individual dealer's website is roughly as good as the worst customer that platform serves.

Add to this: most of these platforms were built by US companies, headquartered in places like Iowa or Colorado, with engineering teams who don't speak French and have never set foot in Lanaudière. The Quebec market is, for them, an edge case. So Quebec gets edge-case treatment.

What modern actually looks like

This is what your dealer website should look like in 2026. Not aspirational — table stakes:

Speed. Sub-2-second page loads on mobile. Page weight under 2 MB. Static rendering or edge caching for the inventory listing pages. Modern image formats with responsive sizing.

Real-time inventory. Sub-15-minute sync from your DMS, ideally event-driven. Sold units off the floor within minutes, not days.

Smart lead capture. Two-field initial forms (contact + name). Progressive disclosure for trade-in, financing, scheduling. Auto-detection of language from the form submission. Auto-routing to the right salesperson based on category. Automated acknowledgment within 60 seconds, in the customer's language.

Real bilingual. Separate URLs per language. hreflang configured. Quebec French content written by people who actually speak it. Language-aware automation through the entire funnel.

Intentional SEO. Page titles and metadata written around real search queries. Local SEO targeted at the regions you actually serve. Schema markup so Google understands your inventory, your business, your reviews.

Mobile as primary. Designed for mobile first. Click-to-call. Click-to-text. Touch-optimized galleries. Sticky CTAs that don't get in the way.

Performance accountability. Monthly reporting on lead volume, response time, conversion rate by source, search visibility, page speed. Numbers that the dealer principal actually sees and acts on.

Direct technical access. When something needs to change, it changes in days — not in the next quarterly platform release.

What this looks like in practice

I've spent the last year working with two pilot dealers (one in Quebec, one in Eastern Ontario) on what this stack should actually be. The short answer: a modern website doesn't have to cost more than a platform contract. In some cases it costs less, because there's no per-feature upcharge.

What it requires is treating the dealer website as a product — owned, iterated, measured — instead of a vendor relationship where the dealer is hostage to whatever the platform ships.

I'm not going to pretend this is a fit for every dealership. A 50-unit-a-year dealer doesn't need any of this. A multi-location dealer doing 800-1500 units a year and competing on a real digital footprint absolutely does.

How to know if you're stuck

Quick checklist. Score yourself honestly. For each item, answer "yes," "no," or "I don't know."

  1. My website's mobile page-load time is under 3 seconds.
  2. Sold units come off my website within an hour of being marked sold in the DMS.
  3. My lead forms have 5 or fewer required fields.
  4. My French content is genuinely adapted, not translated, with separate URLs per language.
  5. My category pages target specific search keywords I deliberately chose.
  6. Leads are routed automatically based on language and unit category.
  7. New leads receive an automated personalized acknowledgment within 2 minutes.
  8. I get monthly performance reports with response time, conversion rate, and search visibility.
  9. I can change a piece of website content within 24 hours without filing a support ticket.
  10. My platform vendor's product roadmap includes things I actually asked for.

If you scored 8+ "yes," your website is in better shape than 95% of the industry. Congratulations.

If you scored 4-7, you're average. Average isn't a position anymore. The dealers eating market share in 2026 aren't average.

If you scored 3 or fewer, you're losing leads and deals every single day and you don't know it because nobody is showing you the data.

The takeaway

Most powersport dealer websites aren't bad because dealers are bad. They're stuck because the platforms serving this industry are structurally unable to move fast, and dealers don't have the leverage or the technical fluency to demand better.

That gap is closing. The dealers who close it first — by either pushing their current vendor harder, switching to something modern, or working with a partner who treats their site as a product — are the ones who'll own the next decade in this business.
